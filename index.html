About our Project

Welcome to our Project!

Our project uses a dataset about stroke prediction acquired from kaggle to predict the chances of getting a stroke. For this prediction, our program utilizes a person's gender, age, marital and smoking status, presence of heart disease or hypertension, work and residence type, bmi, and average glucose level..

Motivation

Our Inspiration!

In the United States, every 40 seconds, someone has a stroke, and every 3 Â½ minutes someone dies of a stroke. 1 in 4 people over the age of 25 in the world will experience a stroke. For people who go to the emergency room within 3 hours of the stroke, they are much more likely to avoid serious disability. Our project is aimed at helping people, especially in the healthcare industry, identify if someone has a higher risk of a stroke, so they can spot warning signs/symptoms of a stroke and access treatment quicker.

In 3 weeks, our final product will have accomplised complete analysis of characteristics that results in stroke, and our product will be to make accurate predictions whether a person will encounter a stroke in the near future based on these chatacteristics.

Machine Learning

Our machine learning models

Regression vs. Classification

Since the problem we are trying to solve is to predict whether a person would encounter a stroke or not, it is classified as a classification problem..


Supervised vs. Unsupervised

Because we fed our model data that was already labeled, this problem is an example of supervised machine learning.


Machine Learning Models

We tested out eleven models (KNN, SVC, RFC, LR, GNB, DTC, SGDC, RC, NC, GPC, MLPC) and found these three to be the best for our data (KNN, SVC, LR). We also ran hyperparameters on these three models to find the best version of these models.

K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN)

For the KNN model we specified n_neighbors and leaf_size as equal to 10. After that, we fit the model to the x_train and y_train and tested it. This allowed us to acquire a minimum observed mean squared error hence validating its accuracy for our data.

Logistic Regression (LR)

For the LR model we specified solver as equal to 'liblinear' and random_state as equal to 0. After that, we fit the model to the x_train and y_train and tested it. This allowed us to acquire the same minimum observed mean squared error hence validating its accuracy for our data.

Support Vector Machine (SVM)

For the SVC model we specified kernel as equal to 'linear'. After that, we fit the model to the x_train and y_train and tested it. This again allowed us to acquire a minimum observed mean squared error hence validating its accuracy for our data.

Comparisons

Deciding on a machine learning model


Accuracy (metric 1)

Accuracy is calculated as (tp + tn)/ (tp + fp _ tn + fn)and you want accuracy to be as close to 1 as possible for the best model.


Mean Standard Error(metric 2)

MSE is calculated by taking the distances of points to the regression line and squaring them. It tells us the average of a set of errors, and we want it to be as close to 0 as possible.

These graphs make it clear that the KNN model is best.

Conclusion

Our findings

Our results show that the variables in our dataset that have a positive correlation with stroke includes females, people with no heart disease, people who work in the private sector, people with hypertension, people who have never smoked, people who are older in age, people who are married, and people with a slightly higher body mass index. These variables indicate that a person may be at an increased risk for getting a stroke. We evaluated that the most accurate machine learning model that resulted in the lowest mean squared error was K Nearest Neighbors (KNN). We hope that our findings will help in aiding the healthcare industry in the prediction and classification of strokes.

Visualizing Count of Categorical Columns

In this collection of bar graphs, there are a number of variables that stick out.While factors like gender and residence didn't seem to have a major impact on strokes, married people and people working in the private sector, did seem to be correlated with a higher rate of strokes. This was also similar in people without hypertension or heart disease, but it was a weaker correlation. These graphs allowed us to see the relationship between strokes and other factors, by also comparing it to the original numbers.

Pie Chart

For people with strokes, there were significantly more cases of strokes in people without heart disease than with it. This displays a negative relationship between heart disease and stroke occurence.

For people with strokes, the majority of them listed their work as "private", but people whose work was "children", they had almost no strokes. Self-employment made up nearly 1/4 of all stroke cases, while government jobs made up about 1/9 of stroke cases.

For all patients in the study, there was a fairly equal distribution of people with rural vs urban residences. This means we can draw conclusions based on the influence of residence since this category is not really skewed.

Hypertension

This dual bar graph compares numerous factors in terms of Urban and Rural residences.For both types of residences, there is a strong positive correlation between strokes and bmi, with people with an "obese" bmi having significantly higher rates of strokes. Overall, while people in their 70s seem to have the highest rate of strokes across all bmi levels and residences, urban people in their 50s with an "obese" BMI tie with the highest amount of strokes (15). Another interesting correlation is that people over 80 seem to have the most strokes in the "overweight" class, but this rate actually decreases in the "obese" class. Conclusively, the data shows that people in urban residences, are older (particularly in their 70s), and have higher BMIs (particularly in the obese categeory) are more at risk for strokes.

3D Model

This collection of graphs depicts the relationships between BMI, heart disease, residence, and strokes. Overall, there is a negative correlation between heart disease and strokes, with people who have heart disease being much less likely to have strokes. However, with BMI, there is a positive correlation, with higher BMI being associated with more strokes. Interestingly, people who had never smoked had the highest rate of strokes, with people who were currently smoking having the lowest. Finally, overall there was a slightly higher rate of strokes in people with urban residences, but there were also many cases when this was flipped.

Scatter Plot

There is a moderately weak, positive correlation between age and BMI, meaning that as age increases so does BMI. This is significant because other graphs show that with increasing age and increasing BMI there is higher stroke risk. The slope of the line is 0.116 with the y-intercept at 23.919, meaning that for every 1 year increase in age, BMI increases on average by 0.116 points. Also, there are a significant number of high BMI outliers, which could be skewing the data - the slope may be too high.

box plot

This boxplot displays the average glucose levels across BMI and the ages of people. There are 4 categories for BMI in the plot (Underweight, Healthy, Overweight, and Obese) and each color in the plot represents an age group. The plot shows that people in their thirties who are underweight have the highest median (average) glucose level of 167.16 compared to the other age groups and BMI groups.

Correlation Heatmap

Correlation heatmaps are a graphical respresentation of the strength of the relationships between multiple variables and whether certain variables have a positive or negative correlation. As the shade of a scale gets darker in the scale, it means that there is little to no correlation between the variables and as the shade of a scale gets lighter, it means that there is a stronger correlation between the variables. The variable "age" on the y-axis and the variable "body mass index (BMI)" on the x-axis has a lighter shade so age and BMI are strongly correlated.

Clustered Bar Graph

The x-axis represents the BMI and the y-axis represents the total strokes. There are 4 clustered groups in the bar graph (Underweight, Healthy Weight, Overweight, and Obese). Each colored bar represents a certain age group in the age category. The bar graph displays that a person who is in their seventies and is obese had 29 strokes in total. This finding suggests that a person who is in their seventies and is obese is at the highest risk of getting a stroke.
